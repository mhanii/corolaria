## Citations and References in Spanish Legal Documents

Spanish legal documents follow a highly codified system for citations and references established by the **Directrices de técnica normativa** (Guidelines on Normative Technique), approved by the Spanish Council of Ministers on July 22, 2005. These guidelines provide formal standards for how legal norms reference one another, ensuring clarity, consistency, and legal security across the entire legal system.[1]

### Understanding Remissions (Remisiones Normativas)

A **remisión normativa** (normative remission) occurs when one legal provision explicitly refers to another provision to incorporate its content or meaning into the referring norm. This is distinct from a bibliographic citation—it creates an active legal connection between norms.[2]

**Express Remissions** are explicit references using formulas such as "de acuerdo con" (in accordance with), "de conformidad con" (in compliance with), or "lo establecido en" (as established in). These clearly indicate that one norm is incorporating content from another.[3]

**Implicit Remissions** occur when one provision references another without using explicit remission language. A provision might state "the regulations will specify the following criteria" without naming the exact regulation, creating an implicit connection that readers must infer from context.[3]

Spanish law distinguishes between **proper remissions** (remisión propia), which reference norms of equal or higher legal hierarchy, and **improper remissions** (remisión impropia), which reference lower-ranking norms. Improper remissions can raise constitutional concerns when they improperly delegate law-making authority to the executive.[4]

Additionally, Spanish legal theory recognizes **static remissions** that reference fixed text already established, versus **dynamic remissions** that reference evolving regulatory content. Dynamic remissions are problematic when a law delegates authority for a regulation to change its own meaning retroactively.[2]

### Citation Rules Within Spanish Legal Documents

The **official guidelines establish a principle of "short and decreasing citations"** (cita corta y decreciente). This means that when citing articles, the citation should decrease in detail as it moves through hierarchical levels. For example, when citing the Constitution, a first reference might state "Artículo 149.1.23.ª de la Constitución Española (CE)," but subsequent citations use only "art. 149.1.23.ª CE".[1]

Within the same document, Spanish legal drafting follows the **principle of citation economy** (economía de cita). This prohibits unnecessary repetition of phrases like "de esta ley" (of this law) or "del presente real decreto" (of this royal decree). Once a document establishes that citations refer to itself, subsequent references become abbreviated. A clause might read: "Artículo 3 establece..." (Article 3 establishes...) rather than repeatedly stating "Artículo 3 de esta Ley establece...".[1]

When citing a series of articles, Spanish legal conventions require **clear indication of both the first and last articles referenced**. Rather than listing each article individually, a drafter might write "artículos 5 a 12" (articles 5 through 12) or "artículos 3, 5 y 7" (articles 3, 5, and 7) when the articles are not consecutive.[1]

### Citation Hierarchy and Format

Spanish law establishes a strict **hierarchical order for citations**, reflecting the constitutional structure:[1]

| Hierarchy Level | Examples | Citation Format |
|-----------------|----------|-----------------|
| Constitutional | Constitution, Statutes of Autonomy | Art. X CE; Art. X EA |
| State Legislation | Laws, Organic Laws | Ley X/YYYY, de DD de MM |
| Delegated Legislation | Royal Decrees-Laws, Legislative Decrees | RDL X/YYYY; RDLEG X/YYYY |
| Regulatory | Royal Decrees, Regulations | RD X/YYYY |
| Administrative | Orders, Resolutions | Orden, Resolución X/YYYY |
| Supranational | EU Legislation | Directive/Regulation designation |
| Judicial | Court Decisions | STC (Constitutional), STS (Supreme), etc. |

**Constitution and Statutes of Autonomy** receive abbreviated citations after first mention. The first reference states the complete name with abbreviation in parentheses: "Constitución Española (CE)" or "Estatuto de Autonomía de Andalucía (EA)." Subsequent references use only the abbreviation: "art. 14 CE".[5][1]

**State and Regional Laws** include in their complete first citation: the type of norm (Ley, Ley Orgánica), the number, year, date, complete title, and the official publication reference. Example: "Ley Orgánica 3/2007, de 22 de marzo, para la igualdad efectiva de mujeres y hombres, Boletín Oficial del Estado, núm. 71, de 23 de marzo de 2007".[5]

**Royal Decrees and Regulations** follow the same format but with the "RD" or "RDL" abbreviation: "Real Decreto 1308/2020, de 5 de febrero, de procedimiento administrativo común".[1]

**Internal Article References** within the same document follow different conventions. On first mention within legislative text, an article might be cited as "el artículo 3 de esta Ley" (Article 3 of this Law), but in subsequent mentions, it becomes simply "art. 3".[1]

### Restrictions on Using Remissions

Spanish legal drafting applies a **principle of restrictive use** for remissions. The guidelines explicitly state that remissions should be reduced to the strictly necessary minimum. This principle stems from the recognition that excessive remissions create confusing and difficult-to-understand legislation.[3][1]

When remissions are deemed necessary, they must include **conceptual mention, not just numeric reference**. Rather than simply writing "art. 7," a drafter should write "el procedimiento establecido en el artículo 7" (the procedure established in article 7) to help readers understand the referenced content without consulting the other article.[3]

**Blank remissions** (remisiones en blanco), where a law delegates authority to create regulatory content without establishing the essential elements in the law itself, are particularly controversial. Spanish constitutional jurisprudence has established conditions for their validity: the remission must be express, the law must contain the essential prohibition or rule, and the regulation must be directly related to that essential content.[2][3]

The guideline against remission proliferation serves a fundamental purpose: **legal security** (seguridad jurídica). When readers cannot understand a norm without consulting multiple other norms, the legal system loses clarity and becomes inaccessible to ordinary citizens.[1]

### Official Publication and Official Gazette References

All citations in Spanish legal documents include references to the **Boletín Oficial del Estado** (BOE, the official state gazette) or the equivalent regional official bulletin where the norm was published. This serves as the authoritative source for the norm's text and is essential for determining when the norm entered into force.[1]

The complete reference includes: the BOE number, the publication date, and when relevant, the page numbers. Example: "Boletín Oficial del Estado, núm. 157, de 2 de julio de 1985, pp. 15.234-15.256".[1]

When citing legislation online, Spanish practice requires including the full URL to the official electronic version, particularly when citing from the official BOE digital portal (boe.es).[5]

### Practical Examples in Spanish Legislation

In actual Spanish legislation, these principles operate as follows. A law might cite another law internally using the economy principle:

> "Sin perjuicio de lo establecido en el artículo anterior, cuando las circunstancias del artículo 5 no concurran, se aplicará el procedimiento especial"[1]

Rather than restating "artículo 5 de esta Ley," the citation assumes readers understand the reference is internal.

A cross-law reference would appear as:

> "de conformidad con lo dispuesto en la Ley Orgánica 6/1985, de 1 de julio, del Poder Judicial"[1]

And would be repeated in abbreviated form in subsequent mentions:

> "según la citada Ley Orgánica 6/1985" or simply "LOPJ"[1]

### Citation of Judicial Resolutions

Spanish legal documents cite judicial decisions using standardized abbreviations:[1]

- **STC** (Sentencia del Tribunal Constitucional) - Constitutional Court Decision
- **STS** (Sentencia del Tribunal Supremo) - Supreme Court Decision  
- **SAN** (Sentencia de la Audiencia Nacional) - National Court Decision
- **STSJ** (Sentencia de los Tribunales Superiores de Justicia) - Superior Court of Justice Decision

These follow the format: "STS 1234/2020, de 15 de noviembre, fundamento jurídico tercero" (STS 1234/2020, of November 15, foundational legal reasoning 3).[1]

### Best Practices for Legal Document Drafting

Spanish authorities recommend several best practices for citations in legal documents:[3]

1. **Minimize remissions**: Use them only when they genuinely improve document clarity, not for mere legislative convenience
2. **Use conceptual language**: Include descriptive mentions of referenced content, not just article numbers
3. **Be consistent**: Follow the established hierarchy and abbreviation conventions throughout the document
4. **Include full references on first mention**: Complete citations enable readers unfamiliar with abbreviated forms to locate the referenced norm
5. **Avoid blank remissions**: If delegating authority to regulations, establish the law's essential provisions first
6. **Check official publications**: Always verify citations against official BOE entries to ensure accuracy

### Evolution and Official Status

These citation conventions are not arbitrary—they reflect Spain's commitment to legal certainty and accessibility. The Spanish Council of State (Consejo de Estado) and the Constitutional Court have repeatedly emphasized that clear, minimally-referenced legal texts better serve the constitutional principle of legal security. The practice of including official publication references, avoiding excessive remissions, and using consistent abbreviations has become deeply embedded in Spanish legislative practice and is now mandatory for all government-generated dispositions.[3][1]
